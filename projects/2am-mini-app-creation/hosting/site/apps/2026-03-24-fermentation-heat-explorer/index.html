Model notes

  • Heat: total heat = sugar consumed (kg) × heat release factor (default 0.556 kJ/g sugar, calibrated for grape must fermentation).
  • Cooling duty: average load = total heat ÷ duration (continuous kW); peak = average × peak factor (sizing headroom). Jacket ΔT framing: UA = 10 W/K per 10,000 L (rough insulated jacket estimate); divide kW by UA to get °C temperature difference the jacket must hold. Adjust UA in the heat-capacity box if your jacket performance differs.
  • Peak factor: red ferments typically 1.8–2.2× average; whites 1.4–1.7×. Reflects the biology of a rising, peaking, then falling fermentation curve.
  • Ethanol & CO₂: stoichiometric sketch from glucose-equivalent conversion (C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2 C₂H₅OH + 2 CO₂). ABV uses 0.059 as a practical conservative coefficient (vs. stoichiometric maximum ~0.065).
  • Unit conversions: °Bx (g sucrose/100 g solution) and °Be (Baumé scale) are converted to g/L using the must density. °Bx ≈ Brix × 10 g/L for typical grape must; °Be ≈ °Bx / 1.8 at typical densities.

How to use this

Pick a ferment type preset or enter your own parameters. The tool estimates total metabolic heat from sugar consumption, then converts it to average and peak cooling loads. The jacket ΔT framing helps you check whether your glycol circuit can sustain the thermal demand.

The adiabatic rise shows how hot the tank would drift if cooling failed entirely — a useful reference point for hot-vintage or高温浸发酵 planning. The CO₂ and ethanol figures are stoichiometric sketches for educational context, not lab measurements.

What this is not: a refrigeration design tool; it ignores vessel wall conduction, latent heat of CO₂ escape, cap management (red ferments), changing must specific heat as ethanol accumulates, and real yeast kinetic fitting. The ΔT / UA model assumes a well-circulated glycol jacket and ignores heating from ambient. For tank-specifying, apply an appropriate safety factor and consult a winery engineer.
