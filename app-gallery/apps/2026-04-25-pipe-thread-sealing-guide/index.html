From hemp and oil to PTFE

Pipe sealing evolved from packing irregular joints to deliberately filling the helical leak path left by threaded fittings.

Pre-industrial jointsHemp, flax, leather, tallow, oil, white lead, and red lead were used to pack and lubricate low-pressure water and gas joints.
19th century standardizationIndustrial plumbing and steam piping drove standardized tapered threads. The taper let male and female threads wedge together under torque.
20th century compoundsPipe dopes blended oils, fillers, solvents, and resins to lubricate, seal voids, and resist vibration or temperature.
Modern PTFE eraPTFE tape became popular because it is clean, chemically resistant, low-friction, and convenient for small threaded fittings.
Key principle

Tapered threads seal by interference, not by the thread crest alone

NPT and similar tapered systems tighten because the thread diameter increases along the fitting. Sealant fills the spiral clearance between crests and roots while also reducing galling so the fitting can reach proper engagement.

PTFE tape is not “glue.” It is a deformable, low-friction film that packs into thread valleys. Too much tape can split female fittings; too little leaves leak paths.

Interactive tape-wrapping guide

Wrap male threads in the same direction the fitting will tighten, normally clockwise when looking at the pipe end.

Recommended wraps
3–4
Start one thread back from the end, overlap by about half the tape width, and pull snug—not stretched thin.
For most 1/2 in metal threads, 3–4 wraps of standard PTFE is a practical starting point. Always follow local code and product instructions.

    Common mistakes & troubleshooting

    Most leaks trace back to incompatible threads, poor preparation, wrong direction, or over/under application.

    Tape unravels during assembly

    It was likely wrapped opposite the tightening direction. Re-clean the male threads and wrap clockwise as viewed from the pipe end.

    Leak after several extra turns

    Stop. More torque can crack female fittings. Disassemble, inspect thread damage, verify standard compatibility, and reapply sealant correctly.

    Valve or regulator contaminated

    Tape may have been started over the first thread. Start one to two threads back so fragments do not enter the flow path.

    Plastic fitting splits

    PTFE reduces friction so over-tightening is easy. Use fewer wraps, hand-tight plus specified turns, and prefer a sealant approved by the fitting maker.

    Stainless threads gall

    Use appropriate anti-galling sealant or high-quality paste. Stainless-on-stainless may seize without lubrication.

    NPT and BSP seem to fit but leak

    They may engage a few turns but have different thread forms/pitches. Use adapters designed for the standards rather than forcing compatibility.

    Safety note: This guide is educational. For gas, oxygen, high-pressure, chemical, potable water, and code-regulated systems, use approved products and follow manufacturer/local requirements.
