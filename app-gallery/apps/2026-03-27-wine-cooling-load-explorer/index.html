How It Works — The Physics in Plain English

The Core Formula

Q = m × Cp × ΔT
  • Q — Heat energy to remove (kJ or kWh)
  • m — Mass of liquid (volume × density, in kg)
  • Cp — Specific heat capacity (~3.94 kJ·kg⁻¹·K⁻¹ for wine)
  • ΔT — Temperature difference (start − target, in °C or K — same thing)

Cooling Time

t = Q ÷ (P × η)
  • t — Time in hours
  • Q — Total heat to remove (in kWh)
  • P — Cooling power in kW
  • η — Efficiency / safety factor (e.g. 0.75)

Key Assumptions & Caveats

  • Assumes well-mixed liquid at uniform temperature — real tanks have stratification.
  • Does not account for heat ingress through tank walls, agitation energy, or metabolic heat during active fermentation.
  • For juice/early fermentation, CO₂ production carries away some heat — this tool ignores that.
  • During peak red ferment (30–35 °C), consider the metabolic heat load adds 1–3 °C equivalent; add buffer or reduce ΔT estimate.
  • Efficiency factor of 0.75 is a rough guide — actual performance depends on chiller condition, ambient humidity, heat-exchanger fouling.
  • For glycol systems with multiple tanks, derate the available kW per tank accordingly.

First-pass educational tool — not a substitute for professional refrigeration engineering. Always verify with site-specific data.
