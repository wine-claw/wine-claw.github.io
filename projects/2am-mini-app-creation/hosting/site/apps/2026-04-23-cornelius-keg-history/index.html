The Cornelius Keg

From Celtic woodcraft to stainless steel soda tanks — the unlikely journey of the keg that homebrewers made their own.

↓ Scroll to explore 2,300 years of keg history

A Chronological History

From oak staves to pressurised stainless — every vessel tells a story.

c. 350 BC — 5th century AD The Wooden Barrel Is Born

Around 350 BC, Celtic craftsmen in central Europe invented the wooden barrel — a vessel that would define beverage storage for over two millennia. Unlike clay amphorae, barrels were stackable, rollable, and surprisingly durable. The Romans, encountering Celtic coopers during their northward expansion, quickly adopted the technology across their empire.[4][5]

Barrel-making — cooperage — became a specialised craft. Oak was the wood of choice: its tight grain held liquid, its flexibility allowed the staves to be bent, and its tannins contributed flavour and antimicrobial properties. The basic design — vertical staves bound by metal hoops — has barely changed in 2,300 years.

5th — 18th century The Barrel Rules the World

Through the Middle Ages and into the early modern period, the barrel was the universal container — for beer, wine, water, fish, gunpowder, nails, and everything else. Guilds of coopers operated in every European city. Barrel sizes were codified into law: the English tierce (42 gallons), the hogshead (63 gallons), the tun (252 gallons).[4]

Beer was stored, aged, and shipped in wooden barrels. Monasteries maintained their own cooperages. Port cities like London, Hamburg, and Bruges ran on barrel logistics. The Guinness brewery in Dublin shipped stout worldwide in wooden casks — a practice that continued into the 20th century.

Late 19th century Metal Enters the Barrel

In 1897, Joseph C. Roth filed US Patent 611,003 for a "Sheet-Metal Beer Keg" — a double-walled, braze-welded steel container insulated with asbestos. It was the first serious attempt to replace wood with metal for bulk beer transport.[3][5]

Roth's keg was heavy, but it solved real problems: metal didn't harbour bacteria, it didn't absorb flavours, and it could withstand internal pressure. Over the following decades, commercial metal beer kegs appeared across Europe and America. Stainless steel — corrosion-resistant, weldable, and food-safe — became the material of choice. Pasteurisation, paired with the sealed metal keg, extended beer's shelf life dramatically.[4]

Early — mid 20th century The Stainless Steel Keg Era

The 20th century saw the stainless steel keg become the global standard for draught beer. German-style DIN kegs (30L and 50L) dominated Europe. American breweries adopted the "sankey" keg with its single-valve spear system. These were heavy-duty industrial vessels — pressurised, reusable, and designed for decades of service.[1]

Meanwhile, the soft drink industry was about to create an entirely different kind of keg — one that would accidentally spark a homebrewing revolution.

1950s — 1970s The Soft Drink Revolution

In the 1950s, Coca-Cola pioneered post-mix dispensing — shipping concentrated syrup to restaurants and mixing it with carbonated water at the point of dispense. This required compact, pressurised stainless steel tanks. Companies like Firestone, the John Wood Company, and Spartanburg Steel Products began manufacturing these beverage transfer tanks.[1][9]

Around 1957, Cornelius Inc. of Osseo, Minnesota — already a maker of soda fountain equipment — introduced its own version. The "Cornelius keg" featured a large removable lid for easy cleaning, two quick-disconnect posts (one for gas, one for liquid), and internal dip tubes. It was designed for premix: the entire soda formulation was pre-carbonated and shipped in the tank.[6]

Two connection standards emerged: Coca-Cola used pin-lock kegs (a bayonet-style fitting with two pins on the gas post and three on the liquid post), while Pepsi used ball-lock kegs (a ball-detent mechanism). The two systems are not interchangeable. Cornelius, Firestone, John Wood, and Spartanburg all made compatible tanks — but the name "Corny" stuck to all of them.[10][11]

1970s — 1990s The Homebrewer's Keg

As the soft drink industry shifted from premix to bag-in-box syrup systems in the 1970s and 80s, thousands of Cornelius-style soda kegs became surplus. They flooded the second-hand market at bargain prices. Homebrewers — who had been bottling their beer one by one — discovered these stainless steel tanks were perfect for kegging homemade beer.[2]

The 5-gallon corny keg was almost ideally sized for a typical 19-litre homebrew batch. The large lid made cleaning easy. The quick-disconnect fittings were simple to use with a CO₂ cylinder and regulator. Ball-lock kegs (Pepsi) and pin-lock kegs (Coke) both found enthusiastic adopters, though ball-lock became the more popular standard among homebrewers due to its wider availability and slightly taller, slimmer profile.[2][10]

By the 1990s, the Cornelius keg was the dominant homebrewing kegging solution worldwide. Manufacturers began producing new corny kegs specifically for the homebrew market — no longer surplus, but purpose-built.

2000s — present The Cornelius Legacy

Cornelius Inc. itself continued to evolve. In 2002, the company — along with 3Wire and Display Technologies — was acquired by Berkshire Hathaway's Marmon Group.[7] It now operates as part of Marmon Foodservice Technologies, still based in Osseo, Minnesota. In 2021, the Cornelius beer dispensing product line was acquired by Italian company Celli Group for its European operations.[8]

Meanwhile, Spartanburg Steel Products sold its keg division in the early 2000s,[12] and Franke subsequently acquired that business.[13]

The corny keg itself remains ubiquitous in homebrewing, craft brewing pilot systems, and even small commercial operations. New stainless corny kegs are manufactured by several companies, and the design — essentially unchanged since the 1950s — has proven remarkably enduring. It is one of the great accidental inventions of the beverage world: a soda industry workhorse that found its true calling in the garage of a homebrewer.

Technical Details Pin Lock vs Ball Lock

Two connection standards, one keg design — an accidental schism from the cola wars.

🔵 Ball Lock (Pepsi)

  • Connection: Ball-detent mechanism
  • Gas post: Identical to liquid post
  • Liquid post: Identical to gas post
  • Identification: Posts are marked/notched or different-sized (7/8″ vs 11/16″ hex)
  • Profile: Taller and slimmer
  • 5 gal height: 24.75″
  • 5 gal diameter: 8.5″
  • Thread (Cornelius): 19/32″-18 TPI
  • Popularity: More common in homebrewing

📌 Pin Lock (Coca-Cola)

  • Connection: Bayonet with pins
  • Gas post: 2 pins
  • Liquid post: 3 pins
  • Identification: Pin count distinguishes gas from liquid
  • Profile: Wider and shorter
  • 5 gal height: 23″
  • 5 gal diameter: 9″
  • Thread (Firestone/JW): 9/16″-18 TPI
  • Popularity: Less common; often cheaper surplus

Anatomy & Operation How a Cornelius Keg Works

Simple, elegant, and remarkably robust — the physics of pressurised dispensing.

Pressure relief valve
Lid & O-ring seal
Liquid dip tube
Gas dip tube (short)
Liquid out port
1

Fill the keg through the large central lid opening. The lid seals with a rubber O-ring and is held in place by a locking clamp. A pressure relief valve on the lid provides safety against over-pressurisation.

2

Pressurise with CO₂ via the gas-in port (short dip tube). The gas enters the headspace above the liquid, creating pressure that forces beer down through the long liquid dip tube.

3

Dispense by opening the liquid-out valve. Pressure pushes the beer up through the long dip tube and out through the disconnect fitting to your tap or faucet. Typical serving pressure: 10–12 PSI.

4

Carbonate by maintaining CO₂ pressure over time. At ~12 PSI and 38°F (3°C), a 5-gallon keg typically reaches full carbonation in 7–10 days. "Force carbing" at 30 PSI for 24 hours is a common shortcut.

5

Clean by depressurising, removing the lid, and washing the interior. The large lid opening makes this far easier than with sankey-style commercial kegs, which require specialised cleaning equipment.

Cornelius Inc. Company History

From a small Minnesota workshop to Berkshire Hathaway — and a name that outlived the product.

1931
Dick Cornelius and N.F. Cornelius Sr. found the company in Osseo, Minnesota. Initial focus: soda fountain and beverage dispensing equipment.
1950s
Coca-Cola pioneers post-mix systems; Cornelius begins developing beverage transfer tanks for the soft drink industry.
~1957
Cornelius introduces the first "Cornelius keg" — a stainless steel beverage transfer tank with removable lid and quick-disconnect fittings. The design that would define a name.
1960s–70s
Cornelius kegs become standard in soda premix distribution. Pin-lock (Coke) and ball-lock (Pepsi) standards established.
1970s–80s
Soda industry shifts to bag-in-box; surplus corny kegs flood the market. Homebrewers begin repurposing them.
2002
Acquired by Berkshire Hathaway's Marmon Group (along with 3Wire and Display Technologies).
Today
Operates as Marmon Foodservice Technologies, still based in Osseo, MN. The "Cornelius keg" name lives on far beyond the company's original product line.
2021
Cornelius beer dispensing product line acquired by Celli Group (Italy) for European operations.
