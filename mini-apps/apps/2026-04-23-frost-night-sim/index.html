What is radiation frost?

On clear, calm nights, the ground and vine surfaces lose heat by long-wave radiation faster than they gain it. Air near the surface cools dramatically — often 5–10°C below the air at 10m height. This "inversion layer" traps cold air at vine level.

Wind machines

A wind machine (fan on a pole) pushes warmer air from the inversion layer down to vine level, mixing it with cold surface air. It needs an inversion of at least 2°C to work. Typically lifts vine-level temp by 1.5–2.5°C.

Sprinkler protection

When water freezes on buds, it releases latent heat (80 cal/g), keeping the bud surface at exactly 0°C — just above lethal. But if water stops, the ice coating can actually make things worse by conducting cold faster.

Australian spring frost context

In Australian wine regions (Barossa, McLaren Vale, Adelaide Hills), spring frosts in September–November can devastate crops. Radiation frosts are the most common type. Vignerons deploy wind machines, sprinklers, and even helicopters on severe nights. A single frost night can cost millions in lost production.
