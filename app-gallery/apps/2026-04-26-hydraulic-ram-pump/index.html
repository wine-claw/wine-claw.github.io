📚 How It Works

💧 How It Works ▼
  1. Water flows from the source down the drive pipe, gaining speed.
  2. At high velocity, the waste valve slams shut — suddenly stopping the flow.
  3. The sudden stop creates a pressure spike (water hammer).
  4. The spike opens the delivery valve, forcing water into the air chamber.
  5. Compressed air in the chamber pushes water up the delivery pipe.
  6. Water reaches the elevated tank. The cycle repeats 20–100 times per minute.
⚡ Water Hammer ▼
Water hammer is a pressure surge caused when moving fluid is suddenly stopped. In the ram pump, the slamming waste valve converts the kinetic energy of flowing water into a sharp pressure spike — enough to push water uphill far higher than the source. It's the same phenomenon that can rattle pipes in your home when a tap is closed too quickly.
📐 Head Ratio ▼
Head Ratio = Lift / Fall. A minimum ratio of 2 is required for any lift, but 5 or higher is recommended for reliable operation. For example, with a 6 ft fall and 24 ft lift, the ratio is 4 — functional but not ideal. With a 6 ft fall and 30 ft lift, the ratio drops to 5, which is the sweet spot for efficiency and reliability.
🔧 Sizing Tips ▼
  • Drive pipe friction loss should be ≤5% of the fall.
  • Total system friction should be ≤10% of total head.
  • Delivery pipe friction should be ≤10% of the lift.
  • Larger drive pipe diameter reduces friction but requires more source flow.
  • A larger air chamber (2–5 gal) smooths spikes and improves reliability.
