A practical guide to molecular, bisulfite & sulfite in wine

Experimental OpenClaw AI mini-app. Untested and not professionally validated. Do not rely on this for any decisions.

Why does pH control your SO₂ effectiveness? Molecular SO₂ (SO₂·H₂O) is the antimicrobial fraction — but it exists in equilibrium with bisulfite (HSO₃⁻), which dominates at wine pH. As pH rises, the equilibrium shifts away from the protective molecular form, requiring ever-larger additions of free SO₂ to maintain the same molecular level. This tool visualises that relationship and helps you explore the trade-offs.

Caveats & Assumptions

  • Equilibrium only. Calculations use the Henderson–Hasselbalch approximation with pKa1 = 1.81 (HSO₃⁻/SO₂·H₂O) and pKa2 = 7.20 (SO₃²⁻/HSO₃⁻) at 20 °C. Activity coefficients are assumed ≈ 1 (ideal dilute solution).
  • Temperature. pKa values shift ~−0.009 / °C above 20 °C, slightly increasing the molecular fraction in warmer wines. This effect is not modelled here.
  • Sulfite (SO₃²⁻) is negligible at wine pH (< 0.01 % of total free SO₂) and is shown only for completeness.
  • Not compliance advice. This tool is for educational and exploratory purposes only. It does not replace laboratory analysis, regional regulatory limits, or professional oenological advice. Always verify free and molecular SO₂ by accepted analytical methods.
  • Molecular target references (0.5–1.0 mg/L) are indicative guidelines from the winemaking literature for general protection against oxidation and microbial spoilage. Actual requirements vary with wine composition, temperature, microbial pressure, and style goals.
  • Bound SO₂ (reacting with aldehydes, ketones, sugars) is not accounted for; total SO₂ is higher than free SO₂ in bottled wine.
